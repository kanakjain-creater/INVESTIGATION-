Step-by-Step Crime Scene Investigation Process

A structured overview of how forensic scientists and investigators work systematically to uncover the truth.

The Investigation Process

Every crime scene investigation follows a disciplined sequence of phases to ensure evidence integrity and legal admissibility.

01
🚧

Securing the Crime Scene

The very first and most critical step is establishing a perimeter using barrier tape or officers to prevent unauthorised access. Contamination by civilians, media, or even unnecessary personnel can destroy fragile evidence like footprints, fibres, or latent fingerprints.

Investigators record the names of every person who enters the scene to maintain a strict chain of custody from the very beginning.

02
📷

Documentation & Photography

Before anything is touched, investigators create a complete visual and written record of the scene. High-resolution photographs are taken from wide-angle (overview), mid-range, and close-up perspectives to capture the scene's context and specific evidence items.

Detailed sketches, 3D scanning, and written notes document the position of every object, allowing investigators to reconstruct the scene later in court with precision.

03
🧤

Evidence Collection

Trained technicians methodically collect physical, biological, trace, and digital evidence using proper tools such as forceps, swabs, and evidence lifters. Each item is handled with gloves to prevent DNA transfer from investigators to samples.

Items are collected in a predetermined pattern (grid, zone, or spiral) to ensure thorough coverage of every area without missing overlooked details in corners or beneath objects.

04
📦

Packaging & Labeling

Each piece of evidence is placed in an appropriate container — paper bags for biological samples (to allow breathing and prevent mould), sealed plastic bags for dry physical items, or rigid containers for fragile objects — and labelled with case number, date, time, and the collecting officer's name.

Proper packaging prevents cross-contamination between samples and preserves evidence until it reaches the laboratory.

05
🔬

Laboratory Analysis

Evidence is transported to a certified forensic laboratory where specialists perform a wide range of tests: DNA profiling, toxicology screens, ballistics matching, fingerprint comparison using AFIS, and chemical analysis of substances.

Laboratory findings are documented in comprehensive reports that detail the methods used, results obtained, and scientific conclusions drawn — all in standardised formats accepted by courts of law.

06
⚖️

Court Presentation

Forensic experts present their findings to the court as expert witnesses. They explain complex scientific concepts in clear, understandable language for judges and juries, while withstanding cross-examination from defence attorneys who may challenge methodology or conclusions.

The credibility of evidence in court depends entirely on how well every preceding step was executed — making flawless documentation and chain-of-custody records indispensable.

Types of Evidence

Forensic investigators encounter several categories of evidence, each handled with specific techniques.

🔑

Physical Evidence

Tangible objects found at the scene — weapons, tools, clothing, glass fragments, and footwear impressions. These items can be directly compared against reference samples to establish links between suspects and the crime location.

🧬

Biological Evidence

Organic material such as blood, saliva, semen, hair, and skin cells that can yield DNA profiles. Biological evidence must be preserved at low temperatures to prevent degradation and contamination that could invalidate test results.

🔎

Trace Evidence

Minute particles transferred between people or objects during contact — fibres, glass shards, paint chips, soil, pollen, and gunshot residue. Trace evidence supports the Locard Exchange Principle: every contact leaves a trace.

💻

Digital Evidence

Data extracted from electronic devices — smartphones, computers, CCTV recordings, GPS logs, and social media activity. Cybercrime specialists use write-blockers and forensic imaging tools to copy data without altering the original storage device.

Tools Used in Investigation

Forensic investigators rely on a precise set of instruments to collect, preserve, and analyse evidence safely and accurately.

🧤
Gloves (Nitrile / Latex)

Worn at all times during evidence handling to prevent DNA transfer from the investigator to the sample, and to protect the officer from potentially hazardous biological material. Gloves are changed between separate evidence items to avoid cross-contamination.

📦
Evidence Bags & Containers

Tamper-evident paper bags (for biological samples) and sealed plastic bags (for dry items) come pre-printed with chain-of-custody fields. Once sealed with a signature or barcode label, any tampering becomes immediately visible, protecting evidence integrity from scene to court.

🪄
Fingerprint Powder & Brushes

Aluminium or carbon-based powders are applied with fine-bristle brushes to reveal latent fingerprints on non-porous surfaces. Black powder is used on light surfaces; white powder on dark ones. Developed prints are photographed and lifted using adhesive tape before being compared to known reference prints via the AFIS database.

🔦
UV Light & Alternate Light Source (ALS)

Ultraviolet and tunable-wavelength light sources reveal substances invisible to the naked eye — body fluids (which fluoresce), trace chemicals, and certain fibres. ALS is a non-destructive first step, allowing investigators to identify and map evidence before collecting it with chemical methods.

📷
Camera & Forensic Imaging Equipment

High-resolution digital SLR cameras with macro lenses, scale rulers, and colour calibration cards are used to produce legally admissible photographic records. 3D laser scanners and photogrammetry software generate accurate crime scene models for courtroom visualisations and accident-reconstruction analysis.
